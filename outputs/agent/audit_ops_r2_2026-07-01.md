# GMEPay+ Operations Gap Audit — Round 2 (day-2 lens)
Date: 2026-07-01
Auditor: Head of Operations

This is a second pass. Round-1 (`audit_ops_2026-07-01.md`) named 22 gaps; the 4.17 ops
backend has since shipped. This pass does NOT repeat round-1 items — it (a) acknowledges what
is now adequately covered by the build, and (b) finds the NEXT layer of gaps that the primitives
themselves have newly exposed. Everything below is grounded in the shipped code, not assumptions.

---

## A. What round-1 + the build now adequately cover (stop worrying about these)

- **Manual recovery primitives now EXIST as real endpoints, not DB edits.** Force-resolve
  UNCERTAIN (`transaction-mgmt POST /v1/transactions/{ref}/resolve`, real FSM, reason+operator+audit,
  idempotent), operator webhook replay (`notification-webhook`, DLQ→PENDING, audited, idempotent),
  and idempotent recon re-run (`settlement-reconciliation POST /recon/rerun`) close round-1 gaps
  #9, #10 at the API layer. Recovery no longer needs an engineering ticket.
- **Emergency containment is real and correctly scoped.** Kill-switch (pause/maintenance/
  suspend partner·scheme·route, audited, immediate/no-4-eyes) PLUS the payment-executor
  operational gate that actually refuses NEW authorizations while letting in-flight + refunds
  complete (fail-open default, 503 retryable). Round-1 #11/#12 are genuinely closed end-to-end —
  the switch now *does* something, which is the part that usually never gets built.
- **Control tower + 360° search exist as an aggregation surface.** `ops-partner-bff
  GET /v1/admin/ops/control-tower` composes in-flight/uncertain, webhook backlog, float headroom,
  health, recon exceptions, operational status, degrading-not-500. Round-1 #1 and #13 have a
  backend. Operator actions are audit-recorded before delegating. Good bones.

## B. Top remaining / newly-exposed operational gaps

1. **The alert loop is emit-only — there is NO consumer, router, or pager.** Grep confirms
   `gmepay.ops.alert` has three emitters (stuck-txn, webhook-backlog, recon-break) and a contract,
   but ZERO subscribers. Alerts fire into a topic nothing reads. Nobody gets woken. The single
   most important day-2 gap: without emit→receive→notify, all the alert work is inert.

2. **No acknowledge / snooze / escalate state machine for alerts.** Even once consumed, there is
   no concept of an alert being owned, ack'd, silenced, or escalated after N minutes unacked. An
   alert you can't ack is an alert that pages the whole team forever or gets muted permanently.

3. **Only 3 of the 6 declared alert types are wired; FLOAT_LOW and DECLINE_SPIKE are phantom.**
   `OpsAlertPayload`'s roster lists STUCK_TXN, UNCERTAIN_AGED, FLOAT_LOW, WEBHOOK_BACKLOG,
   RECON_BREAK, DECLINE_SPIKE — but no emitter exists for FLOAT_LOW or DECLINE_SPIKE. The two an
   ops team cares about most at 3am (a partner running dry, an integration silently declining) are
   named but unbuilt. The contract advertises coverage the platform doesn't have.

4. **Float-low alerting is on a SEPARATE, fragmented channel.** prefunding emits its own
   `prefunding.alert` (EVENT_TYPE_ALERT) via its outbox — NOT `gmepay.ops.alert` FLOAT_LOW. So the
   one float signal that does exist won't land in the unified ops alert stream or the control-tower
   loop. Two alert buses, no convergence. Needs a bridge or a real FLOAT_LOW emitter on the ops topic.

5. **No dedupe / cooldown — every alert re-fires each tick.** Both the stuck-txn sweep and the
   webhook-backlog monitor re-publish on every scheduled pass while the condition persists (both
   build reports flag this as deferred). Once a consumer + pager exist (#1), this becomes an active
   pager-storm defect that will train the team to ignore alerts.

6. **No admin-ui Operations console — it is 100% API, zero clickable surface.** `apps/admin-ui`
   has no `page.tsx` route for ops. Every recovery action (resolve, replay, recon-rerun, pause,
   suspend) is a raw authenticated POST. Under real incident pressure, an operator cannot curl a
   suspend with the right headers/reason — adoption failure. The APIs exist but are un-operable.

7. **Suspend has no controlled RE-ENABLE workflow — unsuspend is a bare toggle.** Suspend is
   (correctly) immediate/one-operator. But un-suspend/resume is equally a single unaudited-intent
   toggle: no maker-checker, no pre-flight health check, no "why is it safe to turn back on",
   no staged/canary re-enable. Turning a compromised partner back ON is riskier than turning it off
   and deserves a guarded workflow. (Config-registry even flags maintenance auto-expiry as deferred.)

8. **No runbook-to-alert linkage and no false-positive tuning loop.** Alert types carry
   type/severity/subjectRef/detail but no runbook URL/ID, and thresholds are static config with no
   feedback on alert precision. Recovery still depends on tribal knowledge (round-1 #18 restated at
   the wiring layer), and nobody can see which alert types are noisy vs. actionable.

9. **BFF RBAC is a header check, not enforcement — the audited actions aren't truly gated.**
   ops-partner-bff guards `ops:operate` only if `X-Gme-Permissions` is present, and ALLOWS when the
   header is absent (dev/gate-off). config-registry ops endpoints have no in-process RBAC at all.
   So the privileged kill-switch/resolve/replay surface is only as safe as an upstream that may not
   set the header. The audit chain records WHO, but nothing yet enforces WHETHER-ALLOWED.

10. **No operator-action audit REVIEW surface / attestation in practice.** Actions are
    audit-recorded (hash chain), but there is still no searchable review UI or periodic attestation
    over force-resolves/suspensions/replays (round-1 #19). Storing ≠ supervising — and now that
    these powerful actions are live and callable, the review gap is materially riskier than in R1.

11. **No kill-switch drill / gameday and no shift-handover or ops MI.** The gate defaults fail-OPEN
    and has never been exercised under load; nobody has rehearsed a real pause→settle-in-flight→
    resume. There is also no shift-handover record (open UNCERTAIN carried, active suspensions,
    in-progress recoveries) and no ops-team MI (MTTR, alert volume, actions/shift, top failing
    partners). The team can act but cannot measure itself or trust the switch under fire.
