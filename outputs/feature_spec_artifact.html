Section 1What GMEPay+ is

GMEPay+ is GME’s cross-border QR-payment hub. It sits between three parties and lets a wallet/remittance partner’s own customer pay a merchant by scanning a QR — across borders and currencies — while GME handles routing, FX, funding, settlement, accounting and regulatory reporting.

Who is involved

  • Wallet / remittance partners (“API clients”) — e.g. GME Remit, SendMN, more later. They own the end customer and their wallet.
  • Their customers — the people who scan and pay. GME never charges them directly; only the partner debits its own customer.
  • QR scheme providers — the local rails: ZeroPay (Korea); Khalti/Fonepay (Nepal); later Alipay, WeChat, QRIS, KHQR.
  • Merchants — the shops that receive money and always get the full payout.
  • GME (the hub) — parses the QR, routes it, prices the FX, funds the payment, settles with everyone, reports to regulators.
  • Regulators — Bank of Korea (FX), KoFIU (AML), Hometax (tax invoices).

How GME earns

Three revenue streams: an FX margin on cross-border payments, a service fee charged to the partner, and a conditional share of the merchant fee (MDR). Every partner × scheme × direction is priced independently.

Section 2Key concepts

  • Partner vs customer vs merchant — partner is GME’s client; customer is the partner’s user who pays; merchant gets paid.
  • MPM vs CPM — MPM: the customer scans the merchant’s QR. CPM: the merchant scans a token from the customer’s wallet.
  • Static vs dynamic QR — static carries no amount (customer types it); dynamic already contains the amount.
  • Inbound vs outbound — inbound = a foreigner pays a Korean merchant; outbound = a customer pays a merchant abroad (e.g. Nepal).
  • Prefunding / float (double prefund) — partners keep a prepaid balance with GME, and GME keeps one with each scheme; both drawn per transaction.
  • Authorize → Confirm (two-phase) — the payment is reserved first (nothing irreversible), then confirmed; GME submits to the scheme only after the partner has charged its customer.
  • Net vs gross settlement — domestic settles net; overseas settles gross with a separate monthly merchant-fee invoice.

Section 3The microservices at a glance

Independent services, each owning its own database, talking only via APIs and events — never a shared database. Features are delivered by these services coordinating.

Section 4End-to-end feature flows

Each feature as a coordinated sequence across the services — what each step does and what it triggers next.

4.1 · Make a payment — inbound (foreign customer → Korean merchant, via ZeroPay)

The flagship two-phase flow. Rules: only the partner charges its own customer, and GME submits to the scheme last (the only irreversible step).

  1. Quote the rate. The partner asks Rate-FX for a locked quote (valid ~15 min).
  2. Start the payment (authorize). The partner sends the request to the Payment Executor, echoing the quoted amount; the Executor re-checks it against Rate-FX.
  3. Check limits. The Executor applies the per-transaction AML cap before anything moves.
  4. Identify the merchant. The Executor asks Merchant & QR Data to resolve the QR to a real, active merchant.
  5. Create a pending transaction. Transaction Mgmt records a PENDING txn, snapshotting rate, margins and merchant fee.
  6. Reserve the partner’s float (hold, not charge). Prefunding reserves payout-cost + FX margin + service fee. Short float declines here — nothing moved.
  7. Check the scheme balance. The ZeroPay adapter confirms GME’s prepaid balance is enough; if short, the authorization is voided before the customer is charged.
  8. Partner charges its own customer. Only the partner debits its customer’s wallet.
  9. Confirm & pay the merchant. The partner confirms; the Executor tells the ZeroPay adapter to submit — paying the merchant. The only irreversible step, done last.
  10. Capture & commit. Prefunding turns the hold into a debit; Transaction Mgmt marks APPROVED and emits “payment approved”.
  11. Book revenue & notify. Revenue Ledger books the margin/fee/split journal; Notification sends the result to the partner.
If it goes wrong: decline → release hold, mark FAILED. Timeout → funds stay held, mark UNCERTAIN for reconciliation. Never confirmed → an expiry sweeper releases the hold.

4.2 · Make a payment — outbound (customer → merchant abroad, e.g. Nepal)

A wallet scans a foreign QR. Routing is decided by the QR itself, with failover across partners.

  1. Scan & classify. The QR Classifier reads the QR’s network (e.g. fonepay.com) + country — routing by the QR, not just the country.
  2. Find the partners. Smart Router returns an ordered candidate list (by priority) of partners that can serve that network.
  3. Try the first partner. The Nepal adapter calls the partner (Khalti) to pay — a single synchronous step returning a reference.
  4. Fail over safely. On a technical failure it first asks that partner whether it already paid; only if it definitely didn’t does it try the next. A business decline is final — no failover, no double-charge.
  5. If all fail. The customer sees a clear “scheme unavailable”.
  6. Record. Transaction Mgmt records the txn; every attempt is logged.
Next step: the Nepal corridor is single-phase today; adding FX (KRW→NPR) and prefunding reserve/capture for it is a documented follow-up.

4.3 · Scan the QR & enter the amount

  • Static QR — no amount encoded; the customer types it, and that’s what’s authorized.
  • Dynamic QR — the amount is in the QR and used as-is.
  • Customer-presented (CPM) — the wallet mints a one-time token (with a prefunding reservation) that the merchant scans.

4.4 · Refund / cancel

  1. Request. The partner or an operator requests a same-day cancel/refund.
  2. Reverse at the scheme. The Executor calls the scheme adapter’s cancel/refund; the merchant payment is reversed.
  3. Restore & record. Prefunding restores the amount, Transaction Mgmt moves to REVERSED/REFUNDED, Revenue Ledger books the mirror reversal at the originally locked rate.

4.5 · Quote the rate & compute amounts (FX)

  • Two-leg, USD-intermediary — collection leg + payout leg, with market±buffer cost rates.
  • Three-tier cascade — scheme KRW rate + GME margin + partner margin.
  • Locked & time-boxed — ~15-min lock; expired quote rejected at confirm; survives restart.
  • GME bears the FX risk in that window; same-currency domestic skips FX (flat KRW fee).

4.6 · Manage prefunding / balances

  • Reserve → capture → release — held at authorize, debited on success, released on decline/expiry; can never go negative.
  • Top-up & credit limit — partners top up in bulk; Config Registry pushes credit limit + AML caps to Prefunding.
  • Low-balance alerts — a threshold breach emits an alert delivered to partner + ops.
  • Two float relationships — partner→GME and GME→scheme tracked independently.

4.7 · Settle & reconcile

  1. Calculate what’s owed. Net (domestic) or gross (international), summed at full precision.
  2. Match against the scheme. Per-merchant match vs the scheme’s files; a mismatch raises a reconciliation exception + ops alert.
  3. Book the journal & residual. Round the liability once at the end; post the tiny rounding difference as a journal entry — exactly once per batch.
  4. Ship files & announce. Generate scheme settlement files; emit “settlement completed”; cross-date refunds netted back.

4.8 · Book revenue & accounting

  • On “payment approved” — Revenue Ledger books a revenue-capture entry (FX margin + service fee) and the fee-share split as balanced double-entry.
  • On “settlement booked” — a journal entry is triggered for the position and the rounding residual.
  • Configurable split — fee-share is config-driven, not hardcoded; every entry balances.

4.9 · Onboard & configure a partner

  1. Draft. An operator fills the onboarding wizard in Config Registry (identity, banking, pricing, schemes, credentials).
  2. Verify (KYB). Config Registry calls the KYB Adapter to screen + verify registration; the verdict is stored.
  3. Approve (4-eyes). A second operator approves; every change is maker-checker controlled + audited.
  4. Issue & wire up. On activation: issue API credentials (Auth-Identity), register the webhook (Notification), push the credit limit (Prefunding).
  5. Set pricing. Per partner × scheme × direction pricing stored, enforcing the cross-border minimum-margin rule.

4.10 · Register schemes & route intelligently

  • Config-driven — each partner is registered with the QR network(s) it serves + a priority. Adding a partner is config, not code.
  • Route by the QR — Smart Router maps a scanned QR’s network to the partner(s) that can serve it, filtered by country/mode/direction.
  • Failover — multiple partners per network are tried in priority order, with the no-double-charge check between hops.

4.11 – 4.16 · The rest, in brief

  • Merchant/QR sync & validation — mirror scheme data; reject unknown/suspended merchants with a precise reason.
  • Partner notifications — signed webhooks, retry with back-off, dead-letter + alert, idempotent delivery.
  • Regulatory reporting — BOK FX (incl. the rate-locked offer-rate field), KoFIU CTR/STR, monthly Hometax invoices.
  • Security & access — signed partner APIs, replay/rate-limit, OIDC operator login, role-based + partner-scoped access, hash-chained audit.
  • Portals — ops dashboards, onboarding & approvals, a sandbox console (incl. a Nepal QR tab), partner self-service.
  • Platform — cloud-agnostic (on-prem / AWS / Azure via config), one database per service, reliable events.

Section 5The money waterfall

Money flows in a layered waterfall; the funding behind it flows the opposite way.

customer
↓
partner float (with GME)
↓
GME scheme float
↓
merchant · full payout
  • Only the partner charges the customer — GME never debits the end customer.
  • GME submits to the scheme last — the irreversible merchant payment happens only after the partner confirms.
  • Double prefund — partner floats with GME; GME floats with the scheme.
  • Refund is a mirror — full reversal at the originally locked rate.

Section 6Coordination non-negotiables

  • Submit last, and only once — the scheme submission is the single irreversible step.
  • Never double-charge on failover — confirm the first partner didn’t already pay before trying the next.
  • Idempotency everywhere — the same payment key never charges twice.
  • One database per service — services talk only via APIs and events.
  • Maker-checker on configuration — every partner/pricing/scheme change needs a second approver, and is audited.
  • Reliable events — business writes + their events recorded together, so revenue, webhooks and reporting never miss one.
